What Are Rogue Planets?

Rogue planets are planetary-mass objects that aren't gravitationally tied to a star. These objects have attracted interest from scientists for years, even though only a few of them have ever been discovered and confirmed.

Where Do Rogue Planets Come From?

While the nature and origin of rogue planets and similar objects are yet to be known, there are many theories about how rogue planets come to be separated from their home stars, including exaggerated orbits that cause the star to lose gravitational control of their planet, stars and planets being thrown out of orbit by collisions or interactions with other stellar bodies, or collisions with other planets in orbit around a star. Because of their nature as planetary-mass objects, rogue planets could even provide clues to the formation process of gas giants like Jupiter and Saturn!

How Do Scientists Find Rogue Planets?

Because rogue planets aren't very luminous in visible light spectra, and are not gravitationally tied to any star, they are very hard to detect. Some telescopes are able to detect the faint infrared light these planets give off, much in the same way they detect brown dwarfs, but scientists mostly rely on gravitational lensing to find rogue planets. The length of time this period of increased brightness lasts allows scientists to discern the size of the planet. Larger rogue planets are easier to detect, as their period lasts for several days, but smaller rogue planets, with Earth-sized masses, are harder to detect, as their periods last for only a few hours. After a planet has been detected, their lack of star parent becomes an advantage to scientists for further studying, as the light from the planet can make its way to Earth, uninhibited by starlight.

How Many Rogue Planets Are There?

Scientists estimate there could be as many as 50 billion rogue planets in our Milky Way galaxy!

Difference Between a Brown Dwarf and a Rogue Planet

There is a very fine line between a brown dwarf and a rogue planet. In fact, this line is so fine that the IAU has classified rogue planets as "sub-brown dwarves"! The main difference between these two objects, however, is mass: rogue planets can be as massive as 12 times the mass of Jupiter, while brown dwarves have a mass between 13 and 80 times that mass.
